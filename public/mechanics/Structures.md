# Structures

Some encounters demand more than a single adversary.

Structures are a framework for creating large-scale entities made of multiple interconnected parts. They can represent ships, vehicles, colossi, living constructs, fortresses, environmental hazards, or any other massive centerpiece encounter.

Inspired by the Colossus framework from _Colossus of the Drylands_, Structures expand the idea into a more flexible system that can represent both hostile and non-hostile entities, adapting to the needs of the story and encounter.

A Structure might be:

- A pirate ship surviving a supernatural storm
- A colossal walking fortress protected by artillery
- A drifting airship powered by unstable ether engines
- A magical train crossing hostile territory
- A dormant titan awakening beneath the earth
- A mobile city defended by automated defenses
- A living organism with multiple vulnerable organs
- A massive environmental hazard the party must survive

Unlike standard adversaries, Structures are divided into multiple Sections. Each Section has its own stat block, features, and purpose within the Structure. These Sections exist inside a shared framework that represents the Structure as a whole.

---

## Structure Framework

A Structure is composed of a primary stat block, called the Framework, alongside multiple smaller stat blocks representing individual Sections of the Structure.

The Framework contains the rules, features, and information that apply to the Structure as a whole, while each Section operates independently with its own features, Hit Points, and mechanics.

Sections can act separately from one another, allowing encounters to evolve dynamically as different parts of the Structure become damaged, disabled, repaired, or destroyed throughout the scene.

Some Sections can become Broken. While Broken, a Section cannot use Actions or Reactions until the condition is cleared.

When a Section marks its last Hit Point, it becomes Destroyed. A Destroyed Section can no longer use any of its features and is considered non-functional for the remainder of the encounter.

Destroyed does not necessarily mean physically removed from the Structure. A destroyed Section may still exist in the fiction while no longer functioning mechanically.

A Structure is usually defeated when its critical Sections are Destroyed, though many encounters instead revolve around narrative objectives, survival, escape, protection, repair, or environmental interaction rather than fully destroying the Structure itself.

---

## Running Structures

Structures are intended to create dynamic, cinematic encounters where the battlefield itself changes over time.

When designing a Structure encounter, consider:

- What is the Structure trying to accomplish?
- Is the Structure aggressive, defensive, environmental, or passive?
- Which Sections are essential?
- Which Sections create tactical decisions?
- What happens when specific Sections are Destroyed?
- How does the environment interact with the Structure?

Structure encounters work best when players are encouraged to move across the Structure, interact with Sections directly, and make meaningful decisions about what to protect, repair, disable, or destroy.

---

## Design Philosophy

Structures follow the same core design philosophy as Daggerheart:

- Keep mechanics streamlined
- Emphasize cinematic storytelling
- Minimize cognitive load
- Encourage collaboration
- Create tactile encounters
- Let the fiction drive the mechanics

Not every Structure needs to be a combat encounter. Some are disasters to survive, puzzles to navigate, or moving locations that evolve throughout the scene.

A Structure should feel like more than a collection of Hit Points. It should feel like an active part of the fiction, shaping the encounter through its scale, mechanics, and presence within the scene.
